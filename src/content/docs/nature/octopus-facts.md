# Octopus Facts

## Three Hearts and Blue Blood

An octopus has three hearts. Two pump blood to the gills, one pumps it to the rest of the body. Their blood is blue because it uses copper-based hemocyanin instead of iron-based hemoglobin, which is more efficient at transporting oxygen in cold, low-oxygen environments.

## Masters of Camouflage

Octopuses can change color, pattern, and even skin texture in milliseconds. They achieve this using chromatophores (pigment cells), iridophores (reflective cells), and papillae (muscle-controlled bumps). Some species can mimic the appearance of other animals like lionfish, flatfish, and sea snakes.

## Each Arm Has Its Own Brain

Two-thirds of an octopus's neurons are located in its arms, not its central brain. Each arm can taste, touch, and make basic decisions independently. A severed arm will continue to respond to stimuli for up to an hour.

## They Edit Their Own RNA

Instead of relying solely on DNA mutations for adaptation, octopuses extensively edit their RNA. This allows them to fine-tune protein production in response to environmental changes, particularly temperature. This level of RNA editing is extremely rare in the animal kingdom.

## Short Lives

Despite their intelligence, most octopus species live only one to two years. The giant Pacific octopus, the longest-lived species, reaches about five years at most.
