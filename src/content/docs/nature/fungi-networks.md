# Fungal Networks

## The Wood Wide Web

Beneath the forest floor, mycorrhizal fungi form vast underground networks that connect the roots of different trees. Through these networks, trees exchange nutrients, water, and chemical warning signals. A single fungal network can span an entire forest.

## How It Works

The fungi colonize tree roots and extend thread-like hyphae far beyond what the roots could reach alone. The tree provides sugars from photosynthesis to the fungus. In return, the fungus delivers phosphorus, nitrogen, and water to the tree.

## Mother Trees

Large, established trees (called hub trees or mother trees) are connected to hundreds of neighboring trees through the fungal network. Research has shown that mother trees preferentially send nutrients to their own seedlings, even recognizing their offspring among unrelated seedlings.

## Chemical Warning System

When a tree is attacked by insects, it can send chemical signals through the fungal network to neighboring trees. The warned trees then begin producing defensive compounds before the insects arrive.

## Scale

A single teaspoon of forest soil can contain several kilometers of fungal hyphae. The largest known single organism on Earth is a honey fungus in Oregon's Blue Mountains, spanning roughly 9.6 square kilometers and estimated to be between 2,400 and 8,650 years old.
