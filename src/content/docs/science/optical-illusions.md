# Optical Illusions

## How They Work

An optical illusion occurs when the visual system processes information in a way that contradicts physical measurement. The brain fills in gaps, assumes lighting conditions, and applies learned patterns, sometimes incorrectly.

## The Checker Shadow Illusion

Two squares on a checkerboard appear to be different shades of grey, but they are identical. The brain compensates for the perceived shadow cast by a cylinder, making one square look lighter than it actually is.

## Motion Aftereffect

Stare at a waterfall for 30 seconds, then look at a stationary object. It will appear to move upward. This happens because neurons tuned to downward motion become fatigued, and the opposing neurons temporarily dominate.

## Why We Fall For Them

The brain processes roughly 11 million bits of sensory information per second but can only consciously handle about 50. Illusions exploit the shortcuts the brain uses to bridge that gap.
